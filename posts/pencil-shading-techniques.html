Five Pencil Shading Techniques for Beginners

19 Jun 26 · 6 min read

Line gets you started; shading makes a sketch feel solid. You do not need twenty techniques — five cover almost every beginner drawing. Practice each on a circle or square before using it on a real subject.

1. Hatching

Parallel lines drawn close together. The closer the lines, the darker the tone. Keep strokes consistent in direction for a clean look, or follow the form of the object so lines wrap around a cylinder or cheek. Use a sharper HB or 2B and light pressure at first.

2. Cross-hatching

A second layer of lines crossing the first — usually at roughly ninety degrees. Builds darker values without smudging. Old masters used cross-hatching for everything from portraits to fabric. Vary the angle slightly on curved surfaces to suggest roundness.

3. Blending

Soft graphite (2B–6B) laid down then smoothed with a finger, tissue, or blending stump. Fast and atmospheric; easy to overdo. Keep one area blended and leave edges crisp for contrast. A kneaded eraser can pull highlights back out of blended zones.

4. Stippling

Dots instead of lines. Slow but precise — excellent for texture (orange peel, rough stone, distant foliage). More dots = darker. Rest your hand on a pivot point and work in small clusters rather than random scatter.

5. Contour shading

Lines that follow the three-dimensional form — like latitude lines on a globe. Powerful for showing volume on arms, apples, and hills. Combine with light hatching for shadows that feel wrapped around the subject.

Putting it together

Most sketches mix techniques: hatching for mid-tone, cross-hatching for deep shadow, a touch of blending on skin or sky, stippling for one textured accent. Try shading one apple five ways — same object, five squares, five methods.

"Light and shade are the great promoters of form." — Leonardo da Vinci
Pressure control Shade from light to dark in layers. Pressing hard immediately flattens the paper tooth and makes erasing difficult. Build value in three passes instead of one heavy stroke.

Common shading mistakes

  • Only outlining — add tone inside the shape, not just around the edge
  • Flat grey everywhere — push darkest darks and leave brightest lights
  • Smudging the whole page — blend selectively, not by default
  • Random line direction — let strokes follow form when possible

Shading is handwriting for volume — repetitive, personal, improvable. One page of spheres and cubes this week will show up in every sketch you draw next month.
