Vincent van Gogh — The Sketchbook Habit

17 Jun 26 · 6 min read

Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890) is famous for swirling starry nights and sunflowers in thick paint — but behind those canvases lies something quieter: hundreds of drawings. He sketched constantly, and his sketchbooks are a masterclass in looking hard and drawing fast.

Drawing before painting

Van Gogh did not treat drawing as a lesser medium. For years he drew almost exclusively — peasants, wheat fields, trees, hands, boots — building the eye and hand he would later bring to colour. He wrote to his brother Theo that he wanted to "get to the point where I can draw anything I see."

What his sketches look like

His drawing style is unmistakable: restless, rhythmic lines that follow the direction of a form rather than tracing its outline. He used reed pens, graphite, and chalk on cheap paper. Proportion was not always perfect — but energy and honesty were always present.

  • Directional hatching — lines follow the curve of a face or the slope of a field.
  • Speed — many sketches feel captured in minutes, not hours.
  • Repetition — he drew the same subjects again and again until he understood them.

The sketchbook as laboratory

Before committing paint to canvas, van Gogh tested compositions in small drawings. A letter to Theo might include a quick sketch of a scene he planned to paint. The sketchbook was where ideas were cheap — mistakes cost only a sheet of paper.

"I tell you, if one keeps on drawing, one draws so much more easily and more quickly." — Vincent van Gogh, in a letter to Theo

What beginners can learn

You do not need to copy van Gogh's style to learn from his habit. Three principles transfer directly:

  • Draw every day — even ten minutes counts.
  • Draw ordinary things — chairs, shoes, trees, hands.
  • Let drawings be unfinished — not every page needs to be a masterpiece.
See the drawings Many museums hold van Gogh's works on paper, including the Van Gogh Museum in Amsterdam. His drawings reveal the thinking behind the paintings — and they are often more approachable for beginners to study.

Van Gogh produced over 1,000 drawings in his lifetime. The lesson is not genius — it is volume. Keep a sketchbook, fill it without judgement, and let the habit do the work.
